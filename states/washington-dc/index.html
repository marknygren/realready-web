Which DC agency replaced DCRA in 2022 as the body responsible for professional licensing, including real estate?

  1. A Office of the Tenant Advocate
  2. B Department of Licensing and Consumer Protection
  3. C Department of Consumer and Business Affairs
  4. D Department of Housing and Community Development
Show answer & explanation +

Correct: B Department of Licensing and Consumer Protection

In 2022, DCRA was reorganized and replaced by the Department of Licensing and Consumer Protection (DLCP), which now oversees professional licensing in DC, including real estate.

Q2 of 20 Licensing & Regulation

Before a DC salesperson can receive their license, they must be affiliated with which type of licensee?

  1. A A licensed DC attorney
  2. B Any licensed salesperson
  3. C A licensed DC broker
  4. D A licensed property manager
Show answer & explanation +

Correct: C A licensed DC broker

DC salespersons must be sponsored by a licensed DC broker. Salespersons cannot operate independently and must work under broker supervision.

  2. B Report the change to DLCP
  3. C Complete an additional 6 hours of CE
  4. D Retake the state licensing exam
Show answer & explanation +

Correct: B Report the change to DLCP

Changes in broker affiliation must be promptly reported to DLCP. The licensee does not need to retake exams or complete additional education for a simple broker change.

  2. B Commingling trust funds with personal funds
  3. C Refusing to represent both parties in a deal
  4. D Charging a commission below market rate

Correct: B Commingling trust funds with personal funds

Commingling trust funds with personal or business operating funds is a serious violation that can result in discipline. Agents are free to set their own commission rates and can decline dual agency.

Q5 of 20 State Practice

Which agency relationship exists when a DC licensee represents only the seller in a transaction?

  1. A Subagent
  2. B Transaction broker
  3. C Dual agent
  4. D Seller's agent

Correct: D Seller's agent

A seller's agent (listing agent) represents only the seller's interests. DC recognizes seller's agents, buyer's agents, and dual agents but does not formally use the term 'transaction broker.'

DC salespersons can only receive compensation through their sponsoring broker. They cannot accept direct payment from clients, other brokers, or any other party to the transaction.

Q12 of 20 State Transactions

DC imposes two separate taxes on real property transfers. Which party typically pays each?

  1. A Seller pays transfer tax; buyer pays recordation tax
  2. B Buyer pays both taxes at closing
  3. C Each party pays half of both taxes
  4. D Seller pays both taxes at closing

Correct: A Seller pays transfer tax; buyer pays recordation tax

DC's two-tax system assigns the transfer tax to the seller and the recordation tax to the buyer. Both are paid at closing when the deed and deed of trust are recorded.

Q18 of 20 State Practice

A buyer submits a $10,000 earnest money deposit. The contract does not specify a deposit deadline. When must the broker deposit it?

  1. A By the next business day
  2. B Within a reasonable time
  3. C Within 7 days of receipt
  4. D Within 3 business days of receipt
Show answer & explanation +

Correct: C Within 7 days of receipt

Per D.C. Code § 42-1704, escrow funds must be deposited within 7 days in a financial institution located within the District. A contract can specify a shorter deadline that the broker must honor, but the 7-day statutory deadline is the default when the contract is silent.

About the exam

More about the Washington DC real estate exam

Washington, DC scores its national and DC-specific portions separately, so a strong national score will not rescue a weak DC portion. The DC Real Estate Commission (DCREC) sits within the Department of Licensing and Consumer Protection, and clearing the DC half on the first try comes down to DC-specific prep.

The DC portion leans heavily on the DCREC's own statutes and rules, including the Real Estate Guaranty and Education Fund, the DCREC advertising requirements at 17 DCMR § 2615, the Tenant Opportunity to Purchase Act, rent control under the Rental Housing Act, and the broader fair-housing protected classes under the DC Human Rights Act of 1977. None of that looks like the material a candidate in a neighboring state studies. A generic real estate practice test won't cut it. You need Washington DC-specific practice questions.

FAQ

Washington DC real estate exam: your questions answered

How many questions are on the DC real estate exam?

The District of Columbia Real Estate Salesperson Examination has 110 scored questions, split into an 80-item national portion and a 30-item DC-specific portion. PSI administers both portions in one sitting at a DC test site, and the two portions are scored separately.

What's the passing score for the DC real estate exam?

75% on each portion separately. PSI uses scaled scoring rather than a literal raw percentage, but plan to clear roughly 60 of 80 on the national side and 23 of 30 on the DC side to be safely above the line. If you pass one portion and fail the other, you only need to retake the failed portion, and you have six months from your first attempt to clear it.

How much does the DC real estate exam cost?

PSI charges $61.50 per examination attempt, and that single fee covers both portions whether you sit them together or retake one. After passing, the DC salesperson license fee is $130 for an initial two-year term per DCREC, with renewal also $130 on the same two-year cycle.

How long is the DC real estate exam?

210 minutes of scored time total, broken into 120 minutes for the national portion and 90 minutes for the DC portion. PSI delivers both portions back-to-back at the DC testing center, and you find out whether you passed before you leave.

Who administers the Washington DC real estate exam?

The District of Columbia Real Estate Salesperson Examination is administered by PSI for the District of Columbia Real Estate Commission (DCREC). You register and schedule your exam date through PSI.

Is the DC real estate exam hard?

The split-portion format is where most candidates trip up. They over-prepare on national real estate principles and underestimate the 30-question DC portion, which is densely DC-specific (DCREC enforcement powers, the Real Estate Guaranty and Education Fund, advertising rules under 17 DCMR, the DC Human Rights Act's expanded fair-housing protections, rent control, and the Tenant Opportunity to Purchase Act). The questions are written to test whether you actually understand DC real estate law, not to trick you. Honest practice on DC-specific questions is the difference between a first-time pass and a $61.50 retake.

What's on the DC real estate exam?

The 80-question national portion covers ten content areas, with contracts (about 17%), practice of real estate (about 13%), general principles of agency (about 13%), financing (about 10%), and real estate calculations (about 10%) carrying the heaviest weights. The 30-question DC portion is heavier still on state law. Half of it tests statutory requirements governing licensee activity (advertising, handling of monies, agency, commissions, fair housing, tenant's right to purchase, property disclosures), and the remaining 50% splits between DCREC's duties and powers (about 25%) and DC licensing rules (about 25%).
